Abstract

We present a three-layer Digital Twin for the predictive maintenance of metal-jet additive manufacturing fleets. A stateless degradation engine computes per-component health updates from physics-informed rate laws; a three-stage simulation harness evolves maintenance policies from constant-interval baselines through self-supervised surrogates to PPO ensembles; and an agentic RAG layer built on LangGraph delivers traceable diagnostics with a hard no-hallucinations contract.

The system is engineered around a single thesis: that grounding is a structural property of the orchestration graph — not an emergent property of the prompt. We report the architecture, the rate-law family, the optimization objective with its $10^9$ infeasibility floor, and the dual-agent diagnostic pipeline that enforces evidence citation and structural validity.

Keywords — digital twin, predictive maintenance, physics-informed simulation, self-supervised representations, proximal policy optimization, agentic RAG, LangGraph, traceability.

§ 1 Model — The Degradation Engine

The stateless brain. A pure function from (state, drivers, dt) to state′.

The degradation engine is the single source of truth for the entire simulation loop. It is deliberately stateless: given a current health vector, an environmental driver vector, and a timestep, it returns the next health vector. No hidden buffers, no accumulators, no implicit memory. Everything that persists across ticks lives in the simulation harness, not the brain. This separation is what allows the same engine to back deterministic Monte Carlo rollouts, RL training environments, and live what-if queries from the diagnostic agent.

1.1 Printer Decomposition & Health Index

The metal-jet binder-jetting platform under study is decomposed into three subsystems covering six tracked components. The decomposition is deliberately coarse: each component must (i) have a credible standalone failure mode, (ii) be addressable by a discrete maintenance action, and (iii) couple to at least one other component physically.

For each component $i \in \{1,\dots,6\}$ we define a scalar Health Index $H_i \in [0,1]$, where $H_i = 1$ denotes pristine condition and $H_i = 0$ denotes confirmed failure. The index is deliberately abstract — it is not a direct physical observable, but a latent quantity inferred from telemetry and reified by the rate law. Health maps to a four-level operational status used throughout the dashboard, the reward function, and the diagnostic agent.

StatusRangeOperational meaning
OK $H > 0.7$ Nominal. No intervention required; component within design envelope.
WARNING $0.4 < H \le 0.7$ Drift detected. Eligible for opportunistic maintenance during scheduled stops.
CRITICAL $0.1 < H \le 0.4$ Active risk. Activates cross-component multipliers; preempt before downstream cascade.
FAILED $H \le 0.1$ Hard stop. Printer offline; availability penalty accrues until repair.

1.2 The Master Rate Law

All six components share a common functional form for their instantaneous degradation rate $\lambda_i$ (units: $\text{health}\,\cdot\,\text{day}^{-1}$). The form is multiplicative across three classes of factors: an intrinsic baseline, an asset-specific calibration, a product of physical driver terms, and a cross-coupling term that captures cascading effects from neighbouring components.

$$ \lambda_i \;=\; \lambda_{0,i} \,\cdot\, \alpha_i \,\cdot\, \underbrace{\prod_{k} g_k\!\left(d_k(t)\right)}_{\text{driver terms}} \,\cdot\, f_{\text{cross},\,i}\!\left(\mathbf{H}_{-i}(t)\right) $$
(1)
$\lambda_{0,i}$
Population-level baseline rate from manufacturer reliability data and field MTBF priors.
$\alpha_i$
Per-asset calibration learned from the SSL encoder (§2.1, Stage 2); captures unit-to-unit variance.
$g_k(d_k)$
Monotone driver responses (humidity, layer count, peak temperature, particulate load…).
$f_{\text{cross},\,i}$
Cross-component coupling — discrete multipliers and continuous feedback (§1.4).

Health updates are integrated with explicit forward Euler, with timestep $\Delta t = 1\,\text{day}$ matched to the dominant driver granularity. Aggressive sub-stepping was rejected as a premature optimization: the rate law is already piecewise-smooth in the drivers and the per-day cap below contains numerical blow-ups at regime transitions.

$$ H_i(t + \Delta t) \;=\; \max\!\Big(\,0,\; H_i(t) - \mathrm{clip}\big(\lambda_i(t) \cdot \Delta t,\;\; 0,\;\; \mathit{MAX\_DH}\big)\Big), \qquad \mathit{MAX\_DH}=1.2 $$
(2)

The cap $\mathit{MAX\_DH} = 1.2$ is intentionally above the natural saturation point ($H \in [0,1]$). Its role is purely numerical: it short-circuits pathological excursions when a learned $\alpha_i$ and a stacked driver multiplier conspire to produce a one-tick rate that would overshoot zero by an unphysical margin. In practice the clip activates on $\sim\!0.03\%$ of ticks across calibrated runs.

1.3 Component Rate Laws (Physics-Informed)

Each component instantiates the master rate law of Equation (1) with a subsystem-appropriate driver product. Drivers are normalized to nominal operating conditions — temperature ratio $T/T_r$, humidity ratio $H/H_r$, recoat-cycle ratio $N_c/N_\text{nom}$, jet-firing-count ratio $N_f/N_\text{nom}$, layer-count ratio $L/L_\text{nom}$, and time-since-maintenance ratio $\tau/\tau_\text{nom}$ — so the exponents read directly as elasticities: the local sensitivity of $\lambda_i$ to a relative change in that driver. Higher exponent $\Rightarrow$ stronger physical coupling.

Reading the exponents

The strongest single sensitivity in the fleet is the heating elements' temperature exponent ($1.8$ on $T/T_r$), consistent with their near-Arrhenius wear-out and the Weibull-shaped tail observed in field-incident data. Second is the TIJ resistors at $1.6$ on $T/T_r$, again temperature-dominated through electromigration. The recoater blade (C1) is the only component with a meaningful humidity elasticity ($0.6$) because powder cohesion is humidity-mediated; the insulation panels carry a weaker but non-trivial humidity dependence ($0.5$) through condensation effects on the panel face.

Throughput drivers split cleanly across subsystems: $N_c$ (recoat cycles) appears only on the recoating components (C1, C2); $N_f$ (jet firings) appears only on the printhead components (C3, C4); the layer count $L$ couples broadly because every layer touches every subsystem. The maintenance-clock ratio $\tau/\tau_\text{nom}$ appears wherever lubricant or seal aging is dominant (C2, C3, C5) — these are exactly the components for which post-maintenance health resets are most consequential, and where a dynamic policy (§2.1, Stage 3) gains the most over a constant interval.

1.4 Coupling & Cascading Failures

Real fleets do not fail one component at a time. A worn blade puts particulates in the chamber; particulates clog nozzles; clogged nozzles drive resistors hotter; hot resistors fatigue the plate. The engine encodes this with two distinct mechanisms — one discrete, one continuous — that together populate $f_{\text{cross},\,i}$ in Equation (1).

Mechanism A — Critical Multipliers (discrete)

When a source component $j$ enters CRITICAL state, a fixed multiplier is applied to the rate of the affected target $i$. The multipliers were tuned against the field-incident database; the most consequential are:

Discrete multipliers (active when source is CRITICAL)
C1contaminates print chamber →C3× 2.0
C3misfires raise local resistor load →C4× 1.7
C6heat loss raises element duty →C5× 1.8
C2guide chatter forces recoat retries →C1× 1.5

Mechanism B — Continuous Physical Cascade

Mechanism A is sharp but brittle: it activates only on a discrete state transition. Real degradation is gradual. We therefore augment it with continuous feedback through shared drivers, where the health of one component modulates a physical quantity that shows up in another's rate law. Two canonical channels:

  • The blade health $H_{C1}$ modulates particulate concentration $c_p$ in the chamber: $c_p = c_{p,0}\,(1 + \gamma\,(1-H_{C1}))$, which feeds directly into $\Lambda_{C3}$ via Equation (4).
  • The insulation health $H_{C6}$ modulates effective heat demand $Q$ on the heating elements: $Q = Q_{0}\,(1 + \delta\,(1-H_{C6}))$, raising the operating $T$ that drives $\Lambda_{C5}$ in Equation (6).
Why both mechanisms Mechanism A reproduces the operational shock a field engineer recognises — “the blade went red and we lost three nozzles by Friday.” Mechanism B reproduces the creep that a constant-interval policy will systematically underestimate. Together they make the policy-search problem genuinely non-trivial: the optimal $\tau$ for any one component depends on the historical state of the others.

§ 2 Simulation — Evolution & Benchmarking

From a global constant to a dynamic ensemble policy — in three deliberate stages.

The simulation harness wraps the degradation engine in a maintenance-decision loop, an episodic accountant for cost and availability, and a comparison rig that lets us benchmark policies of very different shape on a level playing field. The architecture is intentionally evolutionary: each stage exists to answer a specific question that the previous stage left open.

2.1 The Three-Stage Architecture

STAGE 01
Naive Baseline — Optuna constant $\tau$
Global Bayesian search over a single static maintenance interval $\tau_i$ per component. Establishes the floor: what does textbook time-based maintenance achieve?
method · Optuna · TPE sampler
STAGE 02
SSL/SPR Surrogate
Self-Supervised Learning with Self-Predictive Representations on unlabeled telemetry. Yields a state encoder $\phi$ and a learned cost surrogate $\hat V_\theta$ that bypasses full simulation.
method · SPR · transformer encoder
STAGE 03
Agentic RL — PPO Ensemble
A K-policy ensemble trained with PPO on the calibrated simulator. Per-tick decisions, per-component, conditioned on $\phi$. Ensemble vote stabilizes early-training regret.
method · PPO · K=5 · GAE λ=0.95

The “Surrogate Gap”

An honest accounting of Stage 2 reveals an instructive failure: in our initial benchmarks the SSL surrogate, used in isolation as a static policy selector, was outperformed by the Stage-1 Optuna baseline. This is not a bug — it is a structural property of the setup, and worth dwelling on.

For a static policy, the cost landscape is a deterministic function of a low-dimensional parameter vector $\boldsymbol\tau$. The simulator is the cost function. Any learned approximator $\hat V_\theta$ — no matter how clean its representations — introduces an irreducible approximation error $\varepsilon_\theta$ that the direct simulation does not pay. For static $\boldsymbol\tau$, you are competing with yourself with one hand tied behind your back.

The surrogate only earns its keep when the policy class becomes dynamic: when actions are selected per-tick, per-component, conditional on a state vector that lives in a space too large to grid-search. At that point the simulator is no longer a function of a few knobs; it is the inner loop of a credit-assignment problem, and the SSL encoder $\phi$ pays for itself by giving PPO a stable, low-variance state representation. This is the bridge to Stage 3.

2.2 Mathematical Objective & Reward

All three stages optimize the same scalar objective: minimize per-printer expected annual cost subject to a hard fleet availability constraint.

$$ \min_{\pi}\;\; \mathbb{E}_{\,\xi \sim \mathcal D}\!\left[\, C_\text{ann}(\pi;\xi) \,\right] \quad\text{s.t.}\quad A(\pi) \;\ge\; 0.95 $$
(7)

where $\pi$ is a policy (a constant interval, a surrogate-greedy selector, or a PPO actor), $\xi$ is a sampled fleet trajectory, $C_\text{ann}$ aggregates parts cost, labor cost, and lost-throughput cost over a one-year horizon, and $A$ is the time-averaged fraction of fleet hours in non-FAILED status.

The $10^9$ Infeasibility Floor

Constrained optimization is supplied to the optimizer through a piecewise scalarization. Rather than a soft Lagrangian penalty, we use a step function that hard-separates feasible from infeasible regimes:

$$ V(\pi) \;=\; \begin{cases} \,C_\text{ann}(\pi) & \text{if } A(\pi) \ge 0.95, \\[6pt] \,10^{9} \,+\, \lambda_\text{pen}\,\big(0.95 - A(\pi)\big) & \text{if } A(\pi) < 0.95. \end{cases} $$
(8)

The $10^9$ floor is large enough to dominate any plausible cost-side gradient, so the search is structurally forbidden from “buying” cost wins by sacrificing availability. The residual $\lambda_\text{pen}\,(0.95 - A)$ inside the infeasible regime preserves a usable gradient for Optuna and PPO so they can climb out of infeasibility along the steepest local direction. Without that residual the infeasible region is a flat plateau and the optimizer wanders.

§ 3 Interaction — Agentic RAG

A graph, not a prompt. Five nodes, two agents, one contract.

The interaction layer exposes the digital twin to a human operator through natural-language diagnostics. We deliberately rejected the “one-shot RAG” pattern — retrieve-once, generate-once — because it conflates retrieval, reasoning, and validation into a single LLM call where errors at any stage are indistinguishable in the output. Instead we adopt LangGraph Pattern C: Agentic Diagnosis, a five-node directed pipeline with explicit handoffs and an explicit retry edge.

3.1 Architecture — LangGraph Pattern C

N1Intake & route
N2Gatherer (ReAct)
N3Synthesizer
N4Guardrail
N5Emit / alert

Each node has a typed contract on its inputs and outputs. State flows forward; only the Guardrail (N4) can route backward — and only to the Synthesizer (N3), with a bounded retry counter. The graph is intentionally shallow: every transition is auditable, and the entire trace for a diagnostic event fits on a single dashboard row.

3.2 The Dual-Agent Logic

The orchestration splits a diagnostic query into two agents with disjoint responsibilities. The Gatherer is allowed to act on the world (read-only); the Synthesizer is forbidden from acting on the world. This separation is what makes citation enforceable downstream.

Gatherer Agent (N2)

A ReAct loop with a strict, three-tool action space:

# Gatherer tool surface — strictly read-only.
def think(thought: str) -> None: ...
def get_existing_runs(printer_id: str,
                      window: str = "30d") -> list[RunRef]: ...
def query_database(sql: str,
                    params: dict) -> list[Row]: # SQLite historian, read-only role

The Gatherer iterates think → act → observe until it judges it has sufficient telemetry, then hands off a retrieved evidence bundle to the Synthesizer. The bundle is structured: each row carries a run_id, an ISO-8601 captured_at, and a source_query hash. No free text crosses the boundary.

Synthesizer Agent (N3)

The Synthesizer reasons over the bundle and emits a strongly-typed DiagnosticReport. It has no tool surface — it cannot retrieve more data on its own. If it judges the bundle insufficient, its only legal output is a NeedsMoreEvidence object with a justification, which routes back to N2.

$$ \text{DiagnosticReport} \;=\; \big(\,\text{summary},\; \{\,\text{finding}_k\,\},\; \{\,\text{citation}_k\,\},\; \text{recommended\_action}\,\big) $$
(9)

3.3 The “No Hallucinations” Contract

Grounding is enforced by the graph, not requested in the prompt. Three structural mechanisms cooperate:

  • Evidence citation. Every finding in a DiagnosticReport must carry at least one citation object pointing to a row in the bundle. The schema validator rejects reports where any finding lacks a citation; the LLM cannot route past this.
  • Citation-accuracy guardrail. Node N4 re-checks each citation: it confirms the cited run_id appears in the Gatherer's bundle, the timestamp parses as ISO-8601, and the cited values are byte-for-byte present in the corresponding row. Failures route back to N3 with a structured error and a retry counter capped at 3; a fourth failure escalates to a human queue.
  • Proactive monitoring. A FastAPI background task subscribes to the simulator's CRITICAL/FAILED stream and triggers the same five-node graph autonomously. Resulting alerts inherit the same citation contract and are pushed to the operator console over WebSockets.
Design rationale A prompt asking an LLM to “only cite real evidence and never hallucinate” is a hope. A graph that cannot serialize a report missing a citation, and a validator that re-reads the bundle to verify each citation byte-for-byte, is a guarantee. The grounding lives in the topology of the graph and the typed contracts at each edge — exactly the locations where the LLM does not get to vote.

§ 4 Conclusion

One thesis, three layers, zero ungrounded outputs.

We built a digital twin that is honest about what it knows and where it knows it from. The degradation engine is stateless and physics-informed; the simulation harness evolves through three deliberate stages, each answering a question the previous left open; the agentic RAG layer enforces grounding through graph topology rather than prompt incantation. The “surrogate gap” of Stage 2 is not a setback but a signpost: it is precisely the evidence that learned cost models pay for themselves only when the policy class outgrows what direct simulation can grid-search.

Looking forward, three threads remain open. First, the discrete coupling multipliers (Mechanism A) are presently hand-tuned; an obvious extension is to fit them jointly with the SSL encoder against the field-incident database. Second, the PPO ensemble currently votes uniformly; a learned mixture-of-experts gate conditioned on $\phi$ would let specialists dominate in their respective regimes. Third, the agentic RAG retry budget is fixed at three; a learned controller that reads the Guardrail's structured error and chooses between retry, re-gather, and escalate would tighten the loop further without surrendering auditability.

Core takeaway

Grounding is a property of the graph architecture, not just a property of the prompt.
